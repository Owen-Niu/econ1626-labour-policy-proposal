# 3. Policy Options and Evaluation

## Evaluation Criteria

Five criteria are used to assess each policy option:

1. **Effectiveness** — How well the option matches training to actual labour market demand
2. **Equity** — Whether the option provides universal or targeted access across worker demographics
3. **Feasibility** — Practical implementation barriers, including employer engagement requirements
4. **Cost Efficiency** — Value for money, considering deadweight risk and offsetting savings
5. **Political Viability** — Likelihood of legislative support and stakeholder buy-in

## Options Comparison Matrix

| **Criteria** | **Option 1: Sectoral Reskilling** | **Option 2: Learning Accounts** | **Option 3: Skills Guarantee** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Effectiveness | High -- matches training to demand | Moderate -- portable but risks misalignment | High -- preserves firm-specific capital |
| Equity | Moderate -- misses non-unionised workers | High -- universal access | Moderate -- protects incumbents |
| Feasibility | Moderate -- needs employer engagement | High -- builds on existing infrastructure | Lower -- employer opposition likely |
| Cost Efficiency | Moderate -- co-funding improves value | Moderate-low -- deadweight risk | High -- avoids unemployment costs |
| Political Viability | High -- extends existing SWSP | High -- popular entitlement framing | Lower -- seen as regulatory burden |

## Option Descriptions

### Option 1: Sectoral Workforce Solutions Programme (SWSP) Extension

Expand the SWSP to create AI-transition streams that include employer-led plans and one-to-one matching for companies with more than 50 employees. Canada provides an example: more than CAD 100 million in sectoral partnerships by the Government of Canada (2024). This approach leverages existing programme infrastructure while adding dedicated AI reskilling components.

**Strengths:**
- Directly links training to sectoral labour market demand
- Employer co-funding ensures skin in the game
- Builds on proven SWSP architecture

**Weaknesses:**
- May fail to cover workers in non-unionised sectors and small enterprises
- Requires significant employer engagement effort
- Co-funding requirement may exclude resource-constrained firms

### Option 2: Individual Learning Accounts

Portable credits for employees' career training, modelled after Singapore's SkillsFuture programme. Singapore's SkillsFuture has raised the wages of mid-career employees (OECD 2023). Workers receive individual training credits they can spend at accredited providers.

**Strengths:**
- Universal access -- not tied to employer or sector
- Highly portable across jobs and career transitions
- Popular framing as an individual entitlement

**Weaknesses:**
- Australia's VET FEE-HELP scheme has been hampered by weak accreditation
- Risk of credential inflation without employer validation
- Deadweight risk: subsidises training that would have occurred anyway
- Lacks employer accountability -- may produce credentials without employment outcomes

### Option 3: Skills Guarantee (Danish Flexicurity Model)

Follow the Danish model of flexicurity and demonstrate efforts to retrain before reducing staff due to artificial intelligence. The Danish model is a broad package of flexibility, income support and active labour market policies (Madsen 2002). Large enterprises must attempt redeployment before AI-driven redundancy.

**Strengths:**
- Preserves firm-specific human capital
- Avoids unemployment costs through internal redeployment
- Ensures companies internalise social costs of displacement

**Weaknesses:**
- Employer opposition likely -- seen as regulatory burden
- Partial transplantation of one element (without full flexicurity package) carries risk
- Lower political viability due to business community resistance
- Enforcement complexity
